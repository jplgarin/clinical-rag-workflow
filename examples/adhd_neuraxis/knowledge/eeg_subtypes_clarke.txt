Resting EEG Profiles and Subtypes in ADHD

A body of work led by Clarke, Barry, and colleagues characterized resting-state EEG in children with ADHD using eyes-closed and eyes-open recordings. A recurring observation was increased absolute and relative theta power, often most pronounced over frontal and frontal-central regions, accompanied in many children by reduced relative beta or alpha power.

This research also argued against treating ADHD as electrophysiologically uniform. The authors described distinct EEG profiles within ADHD samples, including a maturational-lag pattern of excess slow-wave activity and a separate pattern characterized by excess beta activity in a smaller subgroup. The existence of such subtypes is one reason a single summary ratio can obscure clinically relevant variation between individuals.

For interpretation, elevated frontal theta in an individual child is consistent with the most commonly reported ADHD-associated pattern, but it is neither necessary nor sufficient for the condition. Topography matters: frontal-midline measures such as those at Fz and Cz tend to carry more of the reported group difference than posterior sites.
